About Me

Biography

Landerlei Santos is a geologist with a primary background in geomorphology and Quaternary research, focusing on sedimentary landforms and landscapes. More recently, he has expanded his expertise to include near-surface geophysics, as well as research on planetary surface processes. Currently, he is a Postdoctoral Fellow at the China University of Geosciences, focusing on Earth and Planetary Surface Processes through the integration of GPR data and geomorphic surface analysis.

Landerlei Santos obtained his Bachelor’s degree in Geology from the Federal University of Sergipe (Brazil) in 2012 and a Master’s degree in Geosciences and Environment from São Paulo State University (UNESP, Brazil) in 2015. His Ph.D. research, also at UNESP, focused on geomorphic surface mapping, sedimentary analysis, and luminescence dating to reconstruct the Late Quaternary evolution of the fluvial-aeolian system in the Middle São Francisco River region, Northeast Brazil.

As part of the Latrubesse Research Group, under the program "Large Rivers: Long-Term Basin Evolution, Morphodynamics, and Global Change", Landerlei worked as a Research Assistant at Nanyang Technological University (Singapore), within the Asia School of the Environment (ASE) and the Earth Observatory of Singapore (EOS) between 2019 and 2020.

Throughout his academic journey, during his undergraduate and master's studies (2008–2014), Landerlei was a member of the Georioemar Research Group, led by Professor Luiz Carlos Fontes, and was involved in source-to-sink and marine geology studies. He conducted research on the evolution of the continental margin of the Sergipe-Alagoas sedimentary basin during the Quaternary period. During this time, he participated in multiple oceanographic campaigns, both in shallow marine (continental shelf) and deep-sea environments (>3000 m depth), where he worked on the sampling and description of sediment cores, along with the acquisition of various geophysical datasets. These experiences laid the foundation for his Master’s research, supervised by Professor Edgardo Latrubesse, which focused on the Late Quaternary history of the Vaza-Barris system. The study integrated multiple techniques, including geomorphological mapping of the alluvial and coastal plains, Optically Stimulated Luminescence (OSL) and radiocarbon (C14) dating of alluvial terraces, sediment color analysis, and the acquisition and interpretation of Chirp Sub-bottom Profiler (SBP) sections to assess buried paleovalleys along the continental margin.

During his Ph.D., also under the supervision of Professor Latrubesse, Landerlei contributed to international geomorphology research projects funded by the National Science Foundation (NSF-USA), National Geographic, University of Texas, and University of Florida. These collaboration research included the study of large river systems in the Brazilian and Bolivian Amazon, such as the Solimões, Negro, Amazonas, Tapajós, Beni, Mamoré, and Madeira rivers. He also participated in fieldwork in the Pampean aeolian systems of Argentina, contributing to regional geomorphological reconstructions. Therefore, building a career start with experiences in unique landscapes of South America

My Research Interests

I am seeking to understand how Earth and planetary surface processes, including those induced by human activities, shape the development of landforms and landscapes. Using an integrated approach that combines Geomorphology and Near-Surface Geophysics, my current scientific interest is focused on the São Francisco Basin (Brazil), the Yangtze Basin (China), and the Von Karman Crater (Moon's far side).

Planetary Processes and Landforms

OVERVIEW

A new phase of the space race for scientific exploration of the Moon has begun in this century, and there is an expectation that humans will return to the lunar surface within this decade. Significant scientific advances have been made in recent years through in situ crewed and robotic missions, particularly by the Chinese Chang’E program, including Chang’E-3 (References), Chang’E-4 (References), Chang’E-5 (References), and Chang’E-6 (References). Unprecedented data have been revealed through expeditions to the far side of the Moon, especially by CE-4 and CE-6, which deployed rovers for in situ investigations. These missions have provided high-resolution near-surface geophysical records and physical samples, enabling new studies on the origin and evolution of the lunar surface.

In this context, and aiming to expand my scientific contribution to planetary science, I have been developing research that integrates digital terrain analysis, geomorphological mapping, and interpretation of Lunar Penetrating Radar data to improve our understanding of the distribution and emplacement mechanisms of ejecta deposits on the Moon, particularly in the areas explored by the Chang’E missions.
